1The one thing that decides this regatta

Charleston Harbor is a current venue first and a wind venue second. Three rivers — the Cooper, Ashley and Wando — drain through this harbor, which is why the ebb (outgoing) runs nearly twice as hard as the flood: the rivers add their water to the falling tide. And this weekend is special: Sunday is the new moon, so the tides are building toward springs all weekend. Each day the water moves harder than the day before.

Here's the tactical twist: low water comes about 55 minutes later each day, but the first warning signal barely moves (1:30p, 1:30p, 1:00p). The race window slides across the tide cycle — so each day has a completely different current story:

Friday: you start at slack. Every race after the first is sailed in progressively more flood. What worked in race 1 will not work in race 3.

Saturday: race 1 starts in the last dribble of ebb; the harbor goes slack around 2:10p — likely mid-race or between races — then flood builds hard. The fleet that notices the flip first wins the afternoon.

Sunday: the big one. New-moon spring ebb is still running ~1.3–1.4 kt at the 1:15p start and doesn't go slack until ~3:05p. With the last warning allowed at 3:30p, the final race may be in slack or new flood — the opposite of race 1.

How to verify on the water (do this every day): sail to an anchored mark or the RC boat before the sequence and look at the water dragging past it — the wake points down-current. Check again before every start, not just the first. On transition days (Sat/Sun) the answer changes by the hour.

4The racing area — where the water moves and where it doesn't

Circle A sits between the Middle Ground shoal (west) and the deep Rebellion Reach ship channel (east/northeast), with Crab Bank's shoal to the north and Fort Sumter guarding the entrance to the southeast.

  • Current is not uniform across the circle. It runs hardest in deep water — Rebellion Reach and the channel edges — and noticeably softer over the Middle Ground shoal side and toward Crab Bank. That difference (often 0.5 kt+) is the main strategic lever on this circle.
  • Golden rule: in adverse current, sail the shallow side of the course for relief; in favorable current, get into the deep channel and ride the conveyor.
  • Castle Pinckney is a designated obstruction (SI 10) — so are all commercial vessels, including dredges, ferries and tour boats. The Fort Sumter tour ferry crosses the harbor regularly right through this area; container ships use Rebellion Reach. Stay alert, give way early — they are obstructions, not windshifts.
  • Lay lines move with the water. In cross-current, never bang the up-current layline early — the river will spit you past the mark. Under-stand the side the current carries you toward; over-stand the side it carries you away from.
  • Gates: with current running across the course, one gate mark is up-current. Rounding the up-current gate usually buys a freer first lane on the beat — but check which side has relief first.

NWS Charleston Harbor marine forecast, issued Thursday morning. Re-check each morning — weather.gov/chs → Marine.

  • The daily script: light SW gradient in the morning → the thermal sea breeze fills around midday → peak pressure 1–4 pm, exactly your race window, typically 10–15 kt with more in gusts.
  • The breeze tends to clock right (S → SSW → SW) as the sea breeze matures through the afternoon. A slow persistent right shift rewards keeping right of the fleet late in the day — but on this circle, current relief beats a 5° shift. Solve the current first, then the shift.
  • Saturday is storm-watch day: hot, humid, pre-frontal SW flow with a real chance of afternoon cells. Watch the western sky after 2pm; if thunderheads build, expect the RC to compress the schedule, and get flat fast in gust fronts. Lightning = head in, no debate.
  • Wind-against-current chop: when the SW breeze blows against the ebb (Sunday early, Saturday race 1), the harbor stands up short and steep — worst over the deep channel. Wind-with-flood afternoons flatten it back out.

6Day-by-day playbook

Friday — Race Day 1

SLACK → BUILDING FLOOD
  • Skippers' meeting 11:00a — be there, SI changes post by end of it.
  • Race 1 ≈ slack water: a pure boatspeed/shift race. Sail the breeze, bank a keeper.
  • Races 2–3: flood strengthens under you. Expect the fleet to misjudge laylines — arrive below the mark on the up-current side and let the river deliver you.
  • Pre-start: flood runs along/across the line — take a transit; the line will sag or skew as it builds. Don't get flushed through early (UFD risk) or pinned below.
  • Goal: consistent keepers. No drop until 5 races exist.

Saturday — Race Day 2

EBB → SLACK ≈ 2:10p → FLOOD
  • The flip day. Race 1 in dying ebb, then everything reverses.
  • Check current at every single warning signal — mark wakes, RC anchor line angle. Yesterday's answer is wrong by mid-afternoon.
  • Around slack the harbor goes "honest" briefly — shifts decide. Then flood logic returns: relief upwind on the shoal side becomes channel ride downwind.
  • Storm risk: hydrate early, eat between races, keep an eye west. If a cell approaches, expect shortened course / abandonment signals.

Sunday — Race Day 3

STRONG EBB → SLACK ≈ 3:05p
  • Warning 1:00p with ~1.4 kt of spring ebb still draining — the strongest current of the weekend, plus the steadiest breeze. Steep chop early; hike, vang on, keep the bow out of the back of waves.
  • Beat strategy: hunt relief over Middle Ground / shoal edges when the ebb is foul on the course axis; if it's fair, hold the channel.
  • Time limits matter in current: 75 min for the leader, then a 20-min TLE window — score is finishers + 2. Never, ever quit a bad race.
  • Last possible warning 3:30p ≈ slack/new flood — the final race of the championship is sailed in the opposite water from the first. Stay mentally flexible to the end.

8Rules traps to brief before launching

  • Check-in is mandatory every day: sail past the RC signal boat's stern and hail your sail number until acknowledged, before the first warning. [DP]
  • Stay ashore until flag D flies at the club flagpole with one sound. [DP][NP]
  • Appendix P is live — rule 42 judges on the water. And the SI makes it harsher: the second and every later flag means retire immediately (P2.3 deleted). In this chop the temptation to pump will be huge; one clean technique day beats one yellow flag.
  • DNS without a hearing if you don't start within 5 min of your starting signal — don't drift away from the area between races.
  • Stay clear of other fleets' starting areas when your warning hasn't sounded. [NP]
  • Retiring or skipping a race? You must inform the nearest safety/RC boat.
  • PFD on at all times racing; protest time = 60 min after the RC signal boat docks (jury desk under the Boat House; hearings in the Library Room, North property upstairs).
  • Scoring: max 8 races; 3 = a series; drop kicks in at 5 races.

9Setup & body — hot-harbor checklist

Boat (SW 8–15, chop)

  • Upwind in chop: slightly more twist than flat-water trim — ease vang a touch off max, cunningham just smoothing overbend wrinkles, outhaul a hint fuller for punch through waves.
  • When gusts hit 15+: vang hard, cunningham firm, hike first / pinch second.
  • Tape + spares: tiller universal, mainsheet, clew strap, top batten. Check drain plug daily (everyone forgets on day 3).
  • Write on deck each day: course bearing, slack time, "CHECK CURRENT".

Sailor (88–90°F, water 80°F)

  • 2+ liters on the water with electrolytes; start hydrating at breakfast, not at the dock.
  • Long-sleeve UPF top, hat, sunscreen before launch — June Charleston sun off the water is brutal.
  • Food between races (it can be 3 back-to-back): bars/gels in the cockpit bag.
  • Saturday/Sunday storm kit: watch for the RC's signals, know the abandonment flags, head for the club at lightning. The harbor is no place to argue with a thunderstorm.
